# Domain Glossary

---

# Client

## Meaning

A **Client** represents the external customer account that the organization manages within the platform.

Clients are typically companies or enterprises that hire the organization to run influencer marketing campaigns.  
Clients provide the primary business relationship under which brands, campaigns, and reporting are organized.

A client may contain multiple companies or brands.

---

## Ownership

- A Client belongs to exactly **one Organization (workspace or tenant)**.
- A Client represents a **business relationship**, not a campaign or marketing activity itself.

---

## Relationships

Client → Company (one-to-many)

A client may contain multiple companies or brands.

Client → Campaign (indirect)

Campaigns belong to companies, which belong to the client.

Client → Reports (derived)

Reporting may aggregate campaign performance across all companies and campaigns belonging to the client.

---

# Company

## Meaning

A **Company** represents a brand, business unit, or division that exists within a client.

Companies organize campaigns under specific brands or marketing groups.  
This structure allows enterprise clients to run campaigns across multiple brands while keeping reporting segmented.

---

## Ownership

- A Company belongs to exactly **one Client**.
- A Company acts as the **direct parent entity of campaigns**.

---

## Relationships

Client → Company (one-to-many)

A client may contain multiple companies.

Company → Campaign (one-to-many)

Campaigns must belong to exactly one company.

Company → Reporting (derived)

Campaign reporting may be aggregated by company or brand.

---

# Campaign

## Meaning

A **Campaign** represents a coordinated influencer marketing initiative designed to achieve a specific marketing objective for a brand.

Campaigns organize missions, actions, influencer participation, deliverables, and performance metrics within a defined timeframe.

Campaigns are the primary container for campaign planning, execution, and performance analysis.

---

## Ownership

- A Campaign belongs to exactly **one Company**.
- Campaigns operate within a defined start and end timeframe.

---

## Relationships

Company → Campaign (one-to-many)

A company may run multiple campaigns.

Campaign → Mission (one-to-many)

Campaigns are divided into missions representing stages of execution.

Campaign → InfluencerRating (one-to-many)

Influencers may be evaluated after participating in a campaign.

Campaign → Reporting (derived)

Campaign metrics are aggregated from performance snapshots generated by campaign posts.

---

# Mission

## Meaning

A **Mission** represents a stage or objective within a campaign that groups related actions.

Missions organize campaign execution into phases such as awareness, product demonstrations, or engagement activities.

Missions provide structure for campaign planning and reporting but do not directly assign influencers.

Influencer participation in a mission is derived from the actions they are assigned.

---

## Ownership

- A Mission belongs to exactly **one Campaign**.

---

## Relationships

Campaign → Mission (one-to-many)

A campaign may contain multiple missions.

Mission → Action (one-to-many)

Missions contain one or more actions.

Mission → Influencer participation (derived)

Influencer participation is inferred from assignments to actions within the mission.

Mission → Reporting (derived)

Performance metrics can be aggregated by mission.

---

# Action

## Meaning

An **Action** represents a specific influencer task required as part of a mission.

Actions define the platform, content format, and instructions for the content that influencers must create.

Actions are the smallest planning unit of campaign work.

Influencers are assigned to actions through ActionAssignment records.

---

## Ownership

- An Action belongs to exactly **one Mission**.

---

## Relationships

Mission → Action (one-to-many)

A mission contains multiple actions.

Action → ActionAssignment (one-to-many)

Influencers are assigned to perform the action through assignments.

Action → Deliverable (indirect)

Deliverables are submitted through assignments for the action.

Action → Content Post (indirect)

Posts generated from deliverables fulfill the action.

Action → Performance metrics (derived)

Performance metrics originate from posts created for the action.

---

# Influencer

## Meaning

An **Influencer** represents an individual creator or social media personality who participates in campaigns by producing content on social platforms.

Influencers exist independently from campaigns and can participate in multiple campaigns across different clients and brands.

Influencers maintain a historical record of campaign participation and performance.

---

## Ownership

- Influencers belong to the **organization’s influencer pool**.
- Influencers are not owned by specific campaigns or companies.

---

## Relationships

Influencer → ActionAssignment (one-to-many)

Influencers participate in campaigns by being assigned to actions.

Influencer → ContentPost (indirect)

Posts are created by influencers in response to assignments.

Influencer → InfluencerRating (one-to-many)

Influencers may be evaluated after participating in campaigns.

Influencer → InfluencerNote (one-to-many)

Internal notes may be recorded about influencers.

---

# ActionAssignment

## Meaning

An **ActionAssignment** represents a specific influencer being assigned to perform a particular action.

This entity records participation, assignment status, deadlines, and deliverable tracking.

Assignments define the connection between influencers and campaign actions.

---

## Ownership

- An ActionAssignment belongs to exactly **one Action**.
- An ActionAssignment belongs to exactly **one Influencer**.

---

## Relationships

Action → ActionAssignment (one-to-many)

An action may have multiple influencer assignments.

Influencer → ActionAssignment (one-to-many)

An influencer may have multiple assignments across campaigns.

ActionAssignment → Deliverable (one-to-many)

Assignments produce deliverables submitted by the influencer.

---

# Deliverable

## Meaning

A **Deliverable** represents a required piece of content that an influencer must submit as part of an assignment.

Deliverables may include draft content, final content submissions, or approved assets that fulfill an action requirement.

Deliverables exist before or alongside the final published social media post.

---

## Ownership

- A Deliverable belongs to exactly **one ActionAssignment**.

---

## Relationships

ActionAssignment → Deliverable (one-to-many)

Assignments may require multiple deliverables.

Deliverable → ContentPost (one-to-many)

A deliverable may result in one or more published posts.

Deliverable → Approval workflow (internal)

Deliverables may go through review or approval stages.

---

# Content Post

## Meaning

A **Content Post** represents a published social media post created by an influencer as part of a campaign action.

Posts fulfill deliverables and serve as the source of campaign performance metrics.

Posts contain links to external platform content and metadata about the post.

---

## Ownership

- A Content Post belongs to exactly **one Deliverable**.

---

## Relationships

Deliverable → ContentPost (one-to-many)

A deliverable may generate multiple posts.

ContentPost → PerformanceSnapshot (one-to-many)

Posts accumulate performance metrics over time.

ContentPost → Influencer (indirect)

Posts are associated with the influencer assigned to the deliverable.

---

# Performance Snapshot

## Meaning

A **Performance Snapshot** records the performance metrics of a content post at a specific point in time.

Snapshots allow the system to track how engagement metrics evolve after publication.

Metrics may include impressions, reach, likes, comments, shares, saves, clicks, and other platform-specific data.

---

## Ownership

- A Performance Snapshot belongs to exactly **one Content Post**.

---

## Relationships

ContentPost → PerformanceSnapshot (one-to-many)

A post may have multiple snapshots over time.

PerformanceSnapshot → Campaign reporting (derived)

Campaign, mission, and influencer reporting aggregates metrics from snapshots.

---

# Influencer Rating

## Meaning

An **Influencer Rating** represents an internal evaluation of an influencer’s performance during or after a campaign.

Ratings help track influencer quality, reliability, audience alignment, and collaboration effectiveness.

Ratings provide structured evaluation data for influencer selection in future campaigns.

---

## Ownership

- An Influencer Rating belongs to exactly **one Influencer**.
- Ratings may optionally reference a **Campaign**.

---

## Relationships

Influencer → InfluencerRating (one-to-many)

Influencers may receive multiple ratings across campaigns.

Campaign → InfluencerRating (one-to-many)

Campaigns may produce ratings for participating influencers.

InfluencerRating → Internal evaluation (derived)

Ratings may contribute to influencer scoring or ranking systems.

---

# Influencer Note

## Meaning

An **Influencer Note** represents an internal comment or observation about an influencer.

Notes capture qualitative information such as communication quality, reliability, creative strengths, or concerns.

Notes support relationship management and future influencer selection.

---

## Ownership

- An Influencer Note belongs to exactly **one Influencer**.

---

## Relationships

Influencer → InfluencerNote (one-to-many)

An influencer may have multiple notes recorded over time.

InfluencerNote → User (many-to-one)

Notes are authored by internal users managing the influencer relationship.

## Core Use Cases

# Core Use Case Examples

---

# Campaign Planning

### Use Case: Plan and Configure a Campaign

#### Goal
Create a campaign structure and prepare influencer assignments before campaign execution begins.

#### Steps

1. **Create Campaign**

Campaign manager creates a new campaign for a specific company.

Example:

- Client: Nike  
- Company: Nike Running  
- Campaign: Summer Marathon Push  

System creates a new Campaign record with planning status.

---

2. **Add Missions**

Campaign manager divides the campaign into strategic stages.

Example missions:

- Awareness Launch  
- Product Demo Series  
- Marathon Training Motivation  

Each mission is linked to the campaign.

---

3. **Add Actions**

Campaign manager defines the influencer tasks required for each mission.

Example actions within **Product Demo Series**:

- Instagram Reel: Demonstrate running shoe performance  
- TikTok Video: Share marathon training tips  
- Instagram Story Series: Highlight product features  

Each action includes:

- platform  
- instructions  
- content format  
- deadlines  

---

4. **Assign Influencers**

Campaign manager selects influencers from the influencer pool.

Example influencers:

- Influencer A
- Influencer B
- Influencer C

Influencers are eligible for assignment but not yet connected to specific actions.

---

5. **Create ActionAssignments**

Campaign manager assigns influencers to specific actions.

Example assignments:

- Influencer A → Instagram Reel demo  
- Influencer A → Instagram Story series  
- Influencer B → TikTok training tips  
- Influencer C → Instagram Reel demo  

System creates ActionAssignment records linking influencers to actions.

Campaign is now ready for execution.

---

# Execution

### Use Case: Influencer Completes Campaign Assignment

#### Goal
Execute campaign tasks and capture resulting content.

---

1. **Influencer Accepts Assignment**

Influencer reviews assignment details:

- action description  
- platform requirements  
- deadlines  

Influencer accepts the assignment.

System updates assignment status:

```
assigned → accepted
```

---

2. **Influencer Submits Deliverable**

Influencer submits required deliverable content.

Examples:

- draft video
- draft caption
- content preview

System creates a Deliverable record linked to the ActionAssignment.

---

3. **Team Reviews Deliverable**

Campaign manager reviews submitted content.

Possible outcomes:

- approved  
- revision requested  
- rejected  

If approved, the influencer proceeds to publish the content.

---

4. **Post Published**

Influencer publishes content on the specified platform.

Example:

- Instagram Reel published
- TikTok video posted

System creates a **ContentPost** record containing:

- platform
- post URL
- publish timestamp

The post now becomes eligible for performance tracking.

---

# Analytics

### Use Case: Measure Campaign Performance

#### Goal
Track post performance and evaluate influencer contributions.

---

1. **Platform Metrics Imported**

Background jobs retrieve metrics from social platform APIs.

Metrics may include:

- impressions
- reach
- likes
- comments
- shares
- saves
- clicks

System creates **PerformanceSnapshot** records for each post.

Snapshots capture metrics at specific points in time.

---

2. **Campaign Dashboard Updates**

Analytics layer aggregates metrics across campaign entities.

Metrics are rolled up by:

- action
- mission
- campaign
- influencer

Example dashboard insights:

- total impressions generated
- engagement by mission
- top performing influencers

---

3. **Influencer Performance Evaluated**

Campaign manager reviews influencer performance.

Evaluation factors may include:

- engagement performance
- deliverable quality
- reliability
- timeliness

System creates **InfluencerRating** records tied to the influencer and campaign.

Notes may also be recorded through **InfluencerNote** entries for qualitative observations.

## Multi-Tenant Strategy

# Tenant Isolation Strategy

Tenant isolation defines how data belonging to different organizations (tenants) is separated within the system.

A **tenant** represents an organization or workspace using the platform.  
Each tenant manages its own clients, campaigns, influencers, and campaign data.

The system must guarantee that one tenant cannot access another tenant’s data.

---

# Tenant Model

```
Organization (Tenant)
    ↓
Client
    ↓
Company
    ↓
Campaign
    ↓
Mission
    ↓
Action
    ↓
ActionAssignment
    ↓
Deliverable
    ↓
ContentPost
    ↓
PerformanceSnapshot
```

All operational data ultimately belongs to an **Organization**.

---

# Question 1  
## Does every table contain `organization_id`?

### Recommended Answer

Yes, almost every operational table should contain `organization_id`.

This ensures:

- strict tenant isolation
- simpler permission filtering
- safer queries
- easier analytics segmentation
- simpler debugging and data recovery

### Tables that should include `organization_id`

```
clients
companies
campaigns
missions
actions
influencers
action_assignments
deliverables
content_posts
performance_snapshots
influencer_ratings
influencer_notes
```

### Example schema pattern

```sql
campaigns
---------
id
organization_id
company_id
name
status
start_date
end_date
created_at
```

### Why include it everywhere

Benefits:

- avoids expensive multi-table joins to determine ownership
- simplifies authorization checks
- protects against cross-tenant data leaks
- allows faster tenant-based queries
- enables row-level security if used

---

# Question 2  
## Are cross-client analytics allowed?

### Recommended Answer

Yes, but only **within the same organization**.

Analytics should support:

- campaign comparisons across clients
- influencer performance across campaigns
- platform performance across brands

However, **data must never cross organization boundaries**.

### Example allowed analytics

Within Organization A:

- compare campaigns for Client A vs Client B
- influencer performance across multiple brands
- engagement trends across all campaigns

### Example forbidden analytics

Not allowed:

- comparing campaigns across different organizations
- exposing influencer performance from another tenant

### Enforcement

All analytics queries must filter by:

```
organization_id
```

---

# Question 3  
## Are influencers shared across organizations?

### Recommended Answer

No.

Influencers should be scoped to a specific organization.

Each organization maintains its own influencer pool.

### Reasons

Influencer relationships are often:

- curated by agencies
- negotiated privately
- tied to contracts
- tied to historical collaboration data

Allowing global influencers creates:

- privacy issues
- ownership conflicts
- data leakage risk

---

### Influencer ownership model

```
Organization
    ↓
Influencer Pool
        ↓
Influencer
            ↓
ActionAssignments
```

Influencers can participate in campaigns for:

- multiple clients
- multiple companies
- multiple campaigns

But always **within the same organization**.

---

# Summary

## Tenant Boundary Rules

| Rule | Decision |
|-----|-----|
| Tenant identifier | `organization_id` |
| Every operational table includes tenant id | Yes |
| Cross-client analytics | Allowed within organization |
| Cross-organization analytics | Not allowed |
| Influencers shared across organizations | No |

---

# Key Implementation Principle

Every query that reads operational data must include:

```sql
WHERE organization_id = current_user.organization_id
```

This guarantees tenant isolation across the entire system.

## Analytics Layer Design

# Analytics Layer Design

The analytics layer transforms operational campaign data into structured metrics that power dashboards, reports, and performance analysis.

The analytics system should be separated into four logical layers:

- operational tables
- metric snapshot tables
- aggregation tables
- dashboard queries

This separation improves reporting performance, preserves raw data integrity, and simplifies analytics evolution.

---

# 1. Operational Tables

Operational tables store the **primary application data** used during campaign execution.

These tables represent the source of truth for campaign structure and influencer activity.

Operational tables should **not be optimized for analytics queries**.

---

## Tables

| Table | Purpose |
|------|------|
| clients | stores client accounts |
| companies | stores brands or divisions |
| campaigns | campaign definitions |
| missions | campaign stages |
| actions | influencer tasks |
| influencers | influencer profiles |
| action_assignments | influencer participation in actions |
| deliverables | submitted content assets |
| posts | published social media posts |
| influencer_ratings | influencer evaluations |
| influencer_notes | internal influencer notes |

---

## Example Operational Data Flow

```
Campaign
    ↓
Mission
    ↓
Action
    ↓
ActionAssignment
    ↓
Deliverable
    ↓
Post
```

These records represent **business operations**, not reporting outputs.

---

# 2. Metric Snapshot Tables

Metric snapshot tables capture **point-in-time performance metrics** imported from social platform APIs.

Snapshots preserve historical performance changes for each post.

Snapshots should never overwrite historical data.

---

# Design Principles

The analytics layer should follow these principles:

- raw metrics must always be preserved
- aggregation tables should be rebuilt rather than manually edited
- dashboards should query precomputed tables
- analytics refresh jobs should run asynchronously
- late-arriving platform metrics must update existing aggregates
- all analytics queries must filter by `organization_id` to enforce tenant isolation

```
        ↓

        ↓

        ↓

        ↓
Dashboard reflects updated metrics
```

This layered approach keeps the system:

- scalable
- analytically reliable
- fast for reporting
- easy to maintain
